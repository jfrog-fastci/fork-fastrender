Worth a Thousand Words

This weekend marks the anniversary of a revolutionary invention. Daguerreotype, the first successful form of photography, was announced to the world by the French Academy of Sciences on January 9, 1839 (only a week after the first picture of the Moon was taken). The technique is named for inventor Louis Daguerre, who created it in the 1830s. The discovery’s importance is hard to overstate: Photography changed the way we look at the world, and ourselves. And its impact was immediate, becoming that rare technology, such as the Internet, considered so significant that its intellectual property was gifted to the world.

News from the Steppe

Although the ancient cities of Egypt and Mesopotamia grab archaeological headlines, the vast grasslands of the Steppe can also give us insight into important aspects of history. Here are just a couple of recent finds in the expansive region.

A plague from the Bronze Age

Plague, caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis, is most famous for its role in the European Black Death of the 14th century. Less known is that another strain of that organism wreaked havoc on human populations on the Eurasian Steppe for more than 2,000 years, beginning about 2900 BCE. This version, however, was not borne by fleas. A new study analyzing livestock remains at a Middle Bronze Age site in the southern Ural Mountains suggests that sheep were a likely transmitter of the bacterium to human populations from a still-unknown natural reservoir.

Cinnabar in the Iron Age

The use of pigments as part of funerary rites was common among different cultures. Red pigments were especially popular and none have been more widely used than ocher. But sometimes people opted for something a little more exciting—and dangerous. Lumps of red pigment found in a Scythian grave in southern Ukraine have been identified as cinnabar. The bright red mineral (pictured below) may be beautiful, but it contains high levels of mercury (chemically, it’s mercury sulfide [HgS]) and is dangerous to process.
